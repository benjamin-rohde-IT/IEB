Session 5:
Strategy Formulation &
Managerial Cognition

Understanding how managers interpret uncertainty, form mental models, and build strategy under conditions of rapid technological change.

Core Exam Theme

Why strategy is often constrained by cognition, attention, and the way managers perceive the environment.

What This Session Is Really About

Session 5 focuses on the cognitive foundations of strategy. The main idea is that managers do not react to the environment in a neutral way; they filter reality through mental models, attention, and prior experience.

In digital contexts, this matters even more because technological change creates ambiguity. Managers must decide which signals to prioritize, how to interpret emerging opportunities, and when to revise existing strategy.

The session connects directly to questions about whether incumbents can recognize AI-driven disruption early enough, and whether strategic failure stems from poor analysis or from the limits of managerial cognition itself.

Strategic Insight

Strategy is not only a matter of choosing actions. It is also a matter of seeing the right problems, organizing attention, and building enough flexibility to adapt when the environment changes faster than established routines.

Core Concepts

Managerial Cognition

The mental frameworks managers use to interpret the environment, categorize problems, and form decisions.

Mental Models

Internal representations of how markets, technologies, and organizations work.

Search Processes

How firms explore options, learn from experience, and update strategy under uncertainty.

Environmental Uncertainty

The degree to which managers cannot confidently predict outcomes or know which signals matter most.

Theories & Core Concepts

1. Strategy as a cognitive process

Session 5 argues that strategy is not formed in a vacuum. Managers interpret the environment through prior knowledge, attention, and experience, which means strategic decisions are shaped by how they perceive problems rather than by objective facts alone.

2. Mental models

Mental models are the internal frameworks managers use to understand markets, technologies, and organizational choices. They help managers make sense of uncertainty, but they can also lock firms into outdated assumptions and narrow interpretations.

3. Search processes

Firms do not discover new strategies automatically. They search for options through routines, experimentation, and learning. The quality of this search process determines whether a firm notices emerging threats early enough and adapts in time.

4. Environmental uncertainty

In fast-changing environments, managers often face ambiguity rather than clear risks. This makes it difficult to identify relevant signals, assess their meaning, and choose the right action. Digital transformation increases this challenge because the pace of change is high and the future is hard to predict.

5. Managerial cognition and strategic failure

The session emphasizes that strategic failure can stem not only from poor analysis, but also from cognitive limits: attention constraints, rigid mental models, and overreliance on existing routines. This is especially relevant when firms need to respond to disruptive technologies such as AI.

Core concepts to remember

  • Managerial cognition: the way managers perceive and interpret complex environments.
  • Mental models: internal maps that guide decision-making and shape what managers notice.
  • Search mechanisms: the processes through which firms explore possibilities and learn.
  • Environmental uncertainty: ambiguity about what is happening and what future outcomes are likely.
  • Strategic adaptation: the ability to revise assumptions and action plans when the environment changes.
