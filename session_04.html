How firms build capabilities to sense opportunities, manage deep uncertainty, and orchestrate digital transformation through human-algorithm collaboration and strategic leadership.

Core Exam Theme

Building and sustaining competitive advantage through dynamic capabilities, organizational agility, and wise leadership in the age of AI.

What This Session Is Really About

Session 4 shifts from understanding how firms monetize digital products to understanding how firms build and sustain competitive advantage when facing rapid, unpredictable change. The session explores dynamic capabilities—the organizational and strategic resources firms need to sense opportunities, seize them quickly, and reconfigure their resources.

A critical distinction emerges: managing risk (known outcomes) versus managing deep uncertainty (unknown unknowns). Traditional risk management tools fail in highly uncertain environments like AI adoption and digital transformation. Firms need organizational agility—the ability to adjust strategy and operations cost-effectively without sacrificing efficiency.

The session also reframes AI itself. Rather than treating AI as an autonomous agent, the readings propose that AI is an organizing capability that arises from human-algorithm collaboration. This shift has profound implications for leadership and organizational design.

Strategic Insight

Competitive advantage no longer comes from static assets or capabilities. It comes from the dynamic ability to sense what is changing, seize opportunities quickly, and reconfigure the organization. In the age of AI, this requires not just technological capability, but wise leadership that bridges human, organizational, and technical dimensions.

Core Concepts

Dynamic Capabilities

The ability to sense opportunities, seize them, and reconfigure resources to address changing competitive environments.

Risk vs Uncertainty

Risk has known probabilities; uncertainty has unknown unknowns. Managing them requires different strategies.

Organizational Agility

Balancing efficiency and flexibility to adjust strategy and operations quickly without wasting resources.

Session 4 Core Concepts: Deep Dive

1. Dynamic Capabilities Framework

Dynamic capabilities are the ability of a firm to sense opportunities and threats, seize promising ones quickly, and reconfigure internal and external competencies to maintain competitiveness.

The Three Phases of Dynamic Capabilities:

  • Sensing: Identifying emerging opportunities or threats in the market and technology landscape.
  • Seizing: Mobilizing resources to pursue opportunities and innovate before competitors.
  • Reconfiguring: Restructuring internal and external resources, processes, and partnerships to sustain advantage.

Winners in rapidly changing environments demonstrate timely responsiveness, rapid product innovation, and management capability to effectively coordinate and redeploy both internal and external competencies.

2. Risk vs Deep Uncertainty

This distinction is crucial. Traditional management tools work for risk but fail for uncertainty.

Risk (Known Probabilities):

  • Outcomes are known, probabilities can be calculated.
  • Managed through hedging, insurance, contracts, and risk allocation.
  • Example: currency risk managed through offshore operations or currency futures.

Deep Uncertainty (Unknown Unknowns):

  • Rules, players, and solutions are not fully known in advance.
  • Traditional risk management fails; requires entrepreneurial management and strategic agility.
  • Example: The emergence of smartphones disrupted entire industries in ways that couldn't be predicted or hedged.

The Analogy: Chess (risk) vs Mixed Martial Arts (uncertainty). Chess has finite, known moves and rules. MMA is open-ended—you face unknown opponents with unexpected techniques. Under deep uncertainty, doing the right thing matters more than doing things efficiently.

3. Organizational Agility

Agility is the ability to make timely, effective, sustained organizational change while managing the efficiency-agility tradeoff.

The Agility-Efficiency Tradeoff:

Organizations cannot be equally efficient AND equally agile at all times. Preparing for rapid change requires slack resources, redundancy, and flexibility—all costly.

Good management knows:

  • When agility is needed (high uncertainty environments)
  • When efficiency is sufficient (stable, predictable environments)
  • How to achieve better agility-efficiency tradeoffs than competitors

Strong dynamic capabilities help firms achieve better tradeoffs: they can identify when markets are becoming uncertain and respond faster than competitors, without having wasted resources on unnecessary agility in stable periods.

4. AI as an Organizing Capability (Not Just a Tool)

Traditional view: AI is a tool or a medium that assists human decision-making.
New view: AI is an organizing capability that arises from human-algorithm relations.

Key Properties of AI as Organizing Capability:

  • Connective: AI links humans and algorithms together to accomplish tasks neither could alone.
  • Codependent: Humans and algorithms need each other; neither is autonomous.
  • Emergent: The capability to act arises from the relation, not from the algorithm alone.

Real-world example—Uber:

The "Uber algorithm" is often described as independently matching riders and drivers. In reality, the matching capability emerges from the interactions of drivers, riders, the app, feedback loops, and hundreds of algorithms. No single component has agency; it's the system of relations that creates the capability.

This reframing is critical for leadership. It means:

  • You can't just deploy an AI system and expect it to work. Humans must be reconfigured to work with it.
  • AI adoption is an organizational change challenge, not just a technology deployment.
  • Success depends on effective human-algorithm collaboration design.

5. Leadership Mandate: Wise Digital Transformation

In the age of AI, organizations face a leadership crisis: traditional leaders lack wisdom to navigate digital transformation.

The Three Transformation Challenges:

  • Technological: Understanding AI, data, algorithms, and their capabilities and limitations.
  • Organizational: Restructuring operating models, breaking down silos, enabling cross-functional collaboration.
  • Human: Building culture, inspiring commitment, managing power shifts and resistance to change.

Why Transformation Often Fails:

Many firms dabble in digital transformation with pilots or side projects, but don't commit fully. When new digital units compete with traditional units for resources, traditional units often block adoption. Without committed leadership that "brings the fleet together," transformation falters.

The Imperative: Leaders must have an integrated perspective that combines:

  • Understanding of AI and digital systems (not requiring expertise, but true understanding)
  • Appreciation of organizational and ethical consequences
  • Deep mastery of human leadership—inspiration, culture, managing change
  • Historical perspective and strategic wisdom

6. Strategy in an Era of Abundant Expertise

AI and digital platforms are democratizing expertise, making it cheaper and easier to access specialized knowledge. This fundamentally changes what firms should do internally vs outsource.

The Shift:

Historically, firms grew by internalizing more and more expertise (hiring experts, building departments). Today, firms can access expertise through platforms, partnerships, and AI tools far more cheaply.

Strategic Implication:

Firms must focus on the expertise that differentiates them competitively and use external resources (platforms, APIs, partnerships, AI) for everything else.

Example:

Toyota's competitive advantage comes from expertise in lean manufacturing, not from building its own finance software. Walmart's advantage is distribution expertise, not software engineering. Using external expertise for non-core areas frees resources to deepen core competencies.

How This Connects to Your Synopsis

Novo Nordisk's Dynamic Capabilities

Novo's partnership with OpenAI demonstrates dynamic capabilities in action. Novo senses the opportunity that AI brings to drug discovery and R&D, seizes it through partnership, and reconfigures its research workflows to incorporate AI-driven insights. This isn't just a technology purchase— it requires Novo to build human-AI collaboration capabilities.

Managing Deep Uncertainty in Pharma

Drug development faces deep uncertainty: you don't know if a compound will work, what side effects will emerge, or how regulators will respond. AI can help by improving prediction and experimentation speed, but Novo must maintain organizational agility to adapt as results emerge. This is the pharma firm's dynamic capability challenge.

Likely Oral Exam Questions

What are dynamic capabilities and why do they matter?

How you should answer

Explain that competitive advantage is no longer based on static assets or capabilities, but on the ability to sense opportunities quickly, seize them, and reconfigure resources faster than competitors. Use examples like how firms responded to AI emergence—those that sensed early and reconfigured early gained advantage.

What is the difference between risk and uncertainty, and why does it matter?

How you should answer

Risk has known probabilities (e.g., currency fluctuation); uncertainty involves unknown unknowns (e.g., whether a new technology will disrupt your industry). Traditional tools like hedging work for risk but not uncertainty. For uncertainty, firms need entrepreneurial management, organizational agility, and strategic wisdom— exactly what dynamic capabilities provide.

How should firms think about AI adoption?

How you should answer

Explain that AI is not a standalone technology but an organizing capability that arises from human-algorithm collaboration. Successful AI adoption requires: (1) reconfiguring organizational structures and workflows, (2) designing effective human-AI interaction, (3) building culture and capabilities for change, (4) wise leadership that integrates technical, organizational, and human dimensions.

What does organizational agility mean, and how does it relate to strategy?

How you should answer

Agility is not about being flexible all the time—that's inefficient. It's about knowing when high uncertainty exists and building the capability to respond faster than competitors. Good strategy balances agility and efficiency: invest in agility in uncertain environments (e.g., emerging technologies), pursue efficiency in stable ones. Firms with strong dynamic capabilities achieve better agility-efficiency tradeoffs.

Important Examiner Advice

The examiner will test whether you understand that competitive advantage in the innovation economy comes from the firm's capability to adapt and transform, not from static resources. Avoid describing capabilities as fixed attributes. Instead, show how firms sense changes, seize opportunities through strategic action, and reconfigure resources—especially in your case analysis around Novo Nordisk and AI adoption.
